The History of Rubbish Disposal

The Very First Bins

Extensive archaeological findings demonstrate that ancient humans had a well-structured approach to gathering and discarding their waste. In the Himalayas, excavations have revealed the early use of wheeled carts for transporting surplus woolly mammoth bones (wheeliebins.co.uk, 2013).

When humans began congregating in larger and more sophisticated settlements, from villages to towns to cities, the challenge to deal with all their waste production was dealt with at an increasing scale.

Around 400BC, the Ancient Romans introduced the world’s first municipal landfill. They were also pioneers in creating the first sanitation workers, tasked with hurling waste from the streets into horse-drawn carts. There is even evidence from digs at Pompeii that Romans used a crude form of wheelie bin for waste disposal, though not an effective vehicle for escaping pyroclastic clouds (Davis, 2016; Machemer, 2020).

More Rubbish, Bigger Bins

During the Early Modern Era (1500 AD to 1800 AD), as urban populations continued to swell with people living shoulder to shoulder, the need for systematic waste management became a necessity. In the cities and towns of medieval Europe, people often preferred to drink beer rather than river water, because there was so much garbage polluting them. Beer is still preferable to clean water.

If not for the smell alone, local authorities began collecting and disposing of rubbish on a grand scale, encouraging others to participate. Nevertheless, the invention of disposable items like tin cans, razor blades, and food packaging made the garbage piles bigger and bigger (JMac Property Services, 2017).

One Bin to Rule Them All

In modern times, authorities began distributing waste bins to households and organising routine, professional waste disposal services. The problem remained, however, that early bins were often bulky and difficult to carry to the street for pickup. Even the invention of a lightweight tin bin with handles was unsafe, causing more than a few back injuries.

That was until a rotten apple fell on someone’s head and they thought, “what if we take the wheel, a 6000-year-old invention, and attach it to a bin?” Following this genius realisation, the modern plastic wheelie bin with a flip top lid emerged across Europe in the early 1960s and quickly earned its reputation around the world as the superior bin (wheeliebins.co.uk, 2013).

New Waste Management

Alongside these advancements, automated side loader garbage trucks were invented with interior hydraulic rams to push and compact waste into the rear. This drastically increased load capacity, enabling trucks to empty hundreds of bins in single trips and transport their contents to modern waste facilities for processing (BigTruckRental, 2019; Encyclopedia.com, 2023).

While landfills remain a popular method of waste disposal, other methods include incineration, compaction, biogas generation, composting, and even vermicomposting, which is the process of using worms for the degradation of organic matter into nutrient rich manure (Britannica, 2023).
